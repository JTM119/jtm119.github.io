Overview

Executive Summary

VISIMO, LLC. successfully completed a Phase I proof of concept for Silva, a Machine Learning-powered Progressive Web Application designed to help foresters rapidly assess ecological risk and select appropriate Best Management Practices (BMPs) during both planning and field operations.

The project addresses a critical workforce gap: the US forest management industry faces an 11.5% projected employment decline over the next decade, with retiring senior foresters taking institutional knowledge with them. New foresters require roughly two years of supervised retraining, creating elevated risk of environmental degradation during the transition period.

Context

Problem Statement

A critical aspect of sustainable forestry is the development and implementation of BMPs to protect water quality by preventing sediment from entering water sources and reducing stream bank erosion. Environmental risk assessments currently take anywhere from a few days to 10 weeks to complete. Without intervention, a shrinking labor pool and limited novice experience will result in increased environmental degradation.

Workforce Erosion
Retirement of experienced foresters is disrupting institutional knowledge transfer. New foresters need roughly two years of supervised retraining and often hesitate to act without oversight.
Field Training Gap
Modern university forestry programs have reduced on-site exercises to as little as one week, with many not occurring in actual forests. Direct observation skills are declining.
Time Pressure
Commercial harvest windows are short. The time required to implement BMPs and difficulty of doing so conflicts with operations in critical periods when foresters must gather and sell as much wood as possible.
Assessment Burden
Environmental risk assessments can take 2 days to 10 weeks. BMP implementation weaknesses, including inadequate stream zones and incorrectly installed crossings, erode soil and damage water quality.

Data Synthesis: Bayesian Belief Network

Because public datasets directly linking forestry environmental risk factors to operational conditions are sparse, VISIMO generated synthetic training data guided by Subject Matter Expert (SME) input using Bayesian Belief Networks (BBNs). A BBN is a probabilistic graphical model encoded as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). The network was built in Python using pgmpy, with SMEs providing Conditional Probability Tables (CPTs) via an Excel template.

The full BBN spans more than 50 variables and 14 BMPs across five variable categories. To reduce SME bias, final CPTs were derived from probabilities aggregated across multiple expert opinions, with light Dirichlet smoothing to avoid zero-probability paths.

Figure 1: Simplified structure of the Bayesian Belief Network (BBN). Input categories (soil, geographic, water, operation, equipment) feed into category-level risk factors, which combine to estimate probabilities for the three target outcomes: soil compaction, erosion, and runoff. Recommended BMPs are linked to each outcome. The full network spans 50+ variables and 14 BMPs.

Machine Learning Models

Synthetic data from the BBN was used to train two machine learning approaches. An XGBoost gradient-boosted decision tree model served as a baseline, chosen for its effectiveness with large synthetic datasets. A Graph Neural Network (GNN) was then developed to represent site data hierarchically: a root node aggregates information from category nodes (soil, geographic, water), which connect to variable nodes, which link to relevant BMPs. This structure encodes known associations so the GNN can discover deeper hidden relationships.

"By encoding known associations into the graph, the GNN can uncover deeper hidden relationships and produce more sophisticated predictions" — and crucially, provides a path to stronger performance as real labeled field data accumulates in Phase II.

Results

Model Performance Metrics

Models were evaluated using four metrics. Accuracy measures percentage of correct classifications. Precision measures ability to avoid costly false positives. Recall measures ability to avoid dangerous false negatives. Expected Calibration Error (ECE) measures alignment between predicted probabilities and true empirical frequencies. ECE is the primary metric of interest since Silva presents probability estimates rather than binary predictions.

The true base rate of degradation events in the synthetic data is 20-30%, which depresses accuracy, precision, and recall at a standard 0.5 decision threshold. ECE values in the range of 0.048-0.068 indicate strong probability calibration for early-stage models trained entirely on synthetic data.

The GNN achieves the best ECE of 0.0482 for soil compaction, meaning predicted probabilities deviate from true empirical frequencies by only about 4.8 percentage points on average. This strong calibration is essential for a risk-support tool, since the application presents probability estimates with error bars rather than binary high/low predictions.

True probability distributions from the BBN cluster below 50% for all three risk types, with averages around 22% (soil erosion: 22.5%, soil compaction: 22.3%, soil runoff: 22.1%). This distribution confirms that most subregion scenarios represent low-to-moderate risk and explains why ECE is the most meaningful evaluation criterion rather than accuracy or recall at a 0.5 threshold.

Product

The Silva GIS Application

Silva was built as a Progressive Web Application (PWA) to ensure compatibility across all browsers and operating systems without requiring app store distribution. Key capabilities reflect the workflows observed in the 25 discovery interviews.

Offline First
Users cache map tiles, edit data, and receive updated risk predictions without an internet connection. Edits sync automatically when connectivity returns, fitting real forestry workflows in remote stands.
Interactive Mapping
Users draw polygons to define operation boundaries. Subregions are automatically constructed and pre-populated with data from Web Soil Survey, SSURGO, National Wetlands Inventory, and Maine LiDAR.
Risk Visualization
Color-coded map regions indicate relative risk for each degradation type. Users switch between compaction, erosion, and runoff views from a dropdown. Red regions signal higher risk for the selected type.
BMP Recommendations
Each recommended BMP is shown alongside its predicted percentage improvement in risk score. Users apply BMPs with a single tap and see scores recalculate immediately using updated fields.
Advanced Editing
Users can override automatically pulled data with in-field ground-truth observations for any environmental variable, ensuring the tool adapts as conditions on the ground are verified.
Subregion Splitting
Auto-generated subregions can be divided using custom polylines or polygons to capture fine-grained site variation, with custom naming and independent risk assessment per segment.

Discovery

Stakeholder Findings

VISIMO conducted 25 interviews across diverse backgrounds: practicing foresters in Maine and Washington, procurement technicians, landowners and land managers, educators, and GIS/LiDAR specialists. Three dominant themes emerged and directly shaped both the modeling approach and UI design.

01
Boots on the Ground is Non-Negotiable

Senior foresters use digital tools as a pre-visit triage step and deviate as necessary once on site. Any suggestion that the tool could replace site visits would face significant resistance. As a result, Silva is framed explicitly as a decision support tool rather than a replacement for professional forester judgment. Each recommendation is tied to actionable BMP checklists the forester controls.

02
Explainability and Transparent AI

Stakeholders will only trust tools that clearly show what inputs were used, why a site was scored a certain way, and what BMP options are available. "Black box directives" are unacceptable. Educators raised concerns about younger users accepting outputs uncritically, emphasizing the need to display model recency, validation status, and error bars on predictions. Older foresters indicated distrust of AI unless outputs can be independently verified.

03
Data Pragmatism

Most foresters already use soil maps, DEM/topography, hydrology layers, and LiDAR-derived data as screening inputs. They trust DEM and soils for stable terrain context, but noted vegetation layer data is frequently outdated. A GPS-mapped 75 ft stream buffer might widen to ~200 ft once wet soils are confirmed on site. Silva must accept in-field data updates and reflect company-specific policy constraints, not just state BMP minimums.

Interviewees were cautiously optimistic. Several said the tool would be "very useful" or "well received" if it demonstrably helps meet BMPs, saves planning time, and fits the way work already happens. Commercialization should first target small to mid-size managers and consulting foresters who lack in-house modeling capacity, going to market through trusted channels such as extension programs, CFRU/member networks, and forestry educators.

Looking Ahead

Conclusions and Phase II Roadmap

Phase I established a credible, explainable decision-support core and a field-ready GIS application. All four R&D objectives were completed. The BBN provides a transparent, auditable baseline for risk reasoning while generating synthetic training data. The GNN demonstrates a feasible path to stronger predictive performance as real labeled field data accumulates, with an ECE of 0.0482 for soil compaction representing strong calibration for an early-stage model.

Phase II Priorities

  • Transition from synthetic to real-world field data through collaboration with Dr. Joe Conrad (University of Georgia) for data collection and prediction validation.
  • Classroom testing with University of Maine School of Forest Resources students under Dr. Anil Kizhakkepurakkal, with rigorous performance documentation to support commercialization claims.
  • More flexible stand segmentation, user-supplied regulatory layers, and role-based views for foresters, land managers, and students.
  • Lightweight scenario simulation including generation of hypothetical stands for planning and educational use.
  • Strengthen deep learning approaches (GNN and beyond) while retaining the BBN as an expert-grounded reference baseline that explains why a site is risky.
  • Narrowly scoped commercial pilots that measure planning time saved, avoided wet-site shutdowns, and compliance efficiency to quantify business value before broader rollout.
Project Status With Phase I development complete and a clear plan for Phase II, the project is well positioned to scale into a dependable, explainable tool that helps foresters plan operations and mitigate ecological risks across diverse forest types and geographies.
