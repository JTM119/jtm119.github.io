Verification & Validation Software for Autonomous UAS Certification

The Challenge

Why existing certification fails autonomous UAS

FAA and DoD frameworks were built for human pilots and deterministic aircraft. Machine learning models are nondeterministic, opaque to architectural review, and fail in unpredictable ways. The gap between autonomy capability and certification readiness is widening.

No ML coverage FAA Part 107 certifies pilots, not aircraft AI. DoD standards require verifying software never yields a dangerous result, which is impossible on a nondeterministic ML model without simulation.
Years-long delays Average FAA aircraft certification takes 5 to 9 years and hundreds of required flight hours. A cargo UAS must log 100 safe flight hours per software version, repeated on every update.
BVLOS bottleneck Beyond Visual Line of Sight waivers require case-by-case Secretary of Transportation review, a process too slow for the pace of UAS development.
Rudimentary simulators Existing tools (Gazebo, AirSim, UAV Toolbox) require users to manually design, run, and analyze every test. None offer automated failure injection with post-test analysis or next-test recommendation.
Black-box AI ML performance depends on training data variability, not just architecture. It is virtually impossible to judge safety by examining a model's structure alone.
Late-stage cost Fixing defects discovered in late-stage aerospace development accounts for roughly 95% of all software costs. Current V&V does not catch AI failures until physical flight tests.
Technical Approach

A platform-agnostic Testing Environment

The TE is built on Microsoft AirSim running in Unreal Engine 4. Failures are injected by intercepting sensor data after the simulator generates it but before it reaches the flight controller, keeping the core UAS software untouched and the TE platform-agnostic.

The Proctor

A software object that accepts user-defined success and failure criteria, monitors UAS position, velocity, and collision state in real time, enforces time limits, scores each trial, and recommends parameters for the next test based on prior results.

Failure Injection

Sensor data is intercepted between the simulator and the flight controller. Any corruption type (GPS drift, image blur, pixel obscuration) can be applied at any magnitude without touching the UAS's core software, making tests fully reproducible.

Monte Carlo Scoring

Instead of binary pass/fail, each trial receives a numeric score. Hundreds of trials per condition produce a statistical distribution of outcomes, allowing risk to be expressed as probability rather than a threshold.

Faster Than Real Time

Because no human pilot is needed, AirSim can run many simultaneous trials at accelerated speed. The full 80-trial GPS test suite completed in under one hour, compared to dozens of real-world flight hours for equivalent coverage.

Test Cases

Two failure scenarios, demonstrated end-to-end

Test Case 01 — GPS Failure

Navigation Under Sensor Corruption

The UAS was tasked with flying to a waypoint northwest of a building cluster while avoiding a geofenced no-fly zone. At a random point during flight, GPS data was corrupted for 3 to 20 seconds. Dynamic wind was also applied once the UAS moved 10 meters north of its start.

Two path-planning algorithms were compared across 80 simulated trials each: a direct (shortest path) algorithm and a safe (building-avoidance) algorithm. The TE adaptively increased or decreased wind parameters between trials to isolate the failure boundary.

Key Finding The direct algorithm caused frequent building collisions. The safe algorithm eliminated midair collisions but revealed a rare failure mode at 40–80 m north (before GPS corruption), discovered in under an hour vs. dozens of real flight hours for equivalent data.

Test Case 02 — ML Camera Corruption

Landing Zone Classification Under Degraded Vision

An aUAS with a ResNet50-based vision model was tasked with classifying a landing zone as safe or obstructed, then landing at the primary or a backup site based on its assessment. Six corruption conditions were tested, 50 trials each:

Blur – 9px kernel Blur – 81px kernel 10% pixel obscuration 30% pixel obscuration 50% pixel obscuration No corruption (baseline)

Results were compared against predictions generated by running the ML model in isolation, independent of flight integration.

Key Finding TE-detected failure rates matched all standalone model predictions. Under extreme blurring (81px), the TE found failures the isolated model analysis failed to anticipate, confirming that flight integration reveals failure modes invisible to model-level testing alone.
Applications

Who benefits and how

NASA Applications

Run-Time Assurance (RTA) certification for complex GNC systems, including AI-infused landing safety systems.
System-Wide Safety project support: testing UAS responses to large multi-sensor data streams in emergency scenarios.
Advanced Air Mobility National Campaign: simulating BVLOS operations, vehicle contingencies, and dynamic traffic avoidance.
Stochastic Robustness Analysis (SRA) via Monte Carlo simulation for drone swarm safety research.
Cybersecurity simulation including Position, Navigation, and Timing (PNT) spoofing attacks across multi-vehicle corridors.

Commercial & Government Applications

Small aUAS manufacturers: rapid design iteration and large-scale simulated flight testing at a fraction of physical prototype costs.
FAA and NOAA: modeling low-altitude wind effects and developing weather mitigation strategies below 3,000 feet.
Department of Defense: expediting Certificates of Waiver for UAV NAS access; training, test, and evaluation for surveillance missions.
Emergency services: sizing aUAS fleets for search and rescue, wildfire response, and medical logistics without physical prototypes.
Operators: validating mission profiles in custom UE4 environments (e.g., virtual bridge inspection, forestry monitoring).

Key Findings

What Phase I established

01

Faster-than-real-time simulation allows Monte Carlo test batches at a scale that is impractical with physical flight tests. The GPS scenario yielded 80 trials per algorithm in under one hour.

02

Automated failure injection and post-test analysis surfaces failure modes that traditional V&V and standalone model testing miss. The extreme-blur ML test is the clearest example: failures appeared only when the model was integrated with real flight control software.

03

The numeric scoring system captures degrees of risk, enabling nuanced safety trade-offs (e.g., low-probability high-impact vs. high-probability low-impact failures) that binary certification thresholds cannot express.

04

The platform-agnostic architecture, intercepting sensor data between simulator and flight controller, allows the TE to test any UAS on any sensor type without modifying the software under evaluation.

05

All four project outcomes were completed on time, on budget ($166,319 total), and to NASA assessment standards. The Phase II proposal was submitted alongside this final report.
