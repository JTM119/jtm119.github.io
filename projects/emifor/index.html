The Problem

Carbon capture at scale demands real-time emission insight

Amine-based post-combustion carbon capture (PCC) scrubs flue gas with an amine solution, capturing approximately 90% of CO2. But amines degrade over time, producing carcinogenic by-products like nitrosamines and nitramines that must be carefully managed.

Today, emission tracking is largely manual: process simulators and spreadsheets, inconsistent sensor data, and no tool that can forecast what emissions will look like in the next 30 minutes. The ability to forecast in real time is critical for safe, compliant plant operation.

EmiFor fills that gap: a forecasting framework grounded in real plant physics, validated against two international pilot plants, and delivered through an interface any operator can use.

Amine PCC Process

01
Absorber
Flue gas contacts amine solution; CO2 is captured. A water wash section limits amine carryover into the stack.
02
Regenerator
Steam heats the rich solvent to release concentrated CO2, which is compressed and piped to a storage site.
03
Solvent Degradation
Thermal and oxidative cycling degrades the amine, generating nitrosamines, nitramines, and other regulated emission compounds.
04
Emission Monitoring
AMP and other species are measured in the stack. EmiFor forecasts these 30 minutes ahead from five process variables sampled at 5-minute intervals.

Technical Approach

From sparse observations to physics-informed forecasts

Three interconnected activities formed the technical core of Phase I: understanding and augmenting available data, developing two families of deep learning models, and validating predictive and analytical capabilities on real plant data.

Data Sources

Technology Centre Mongstad (TCM), Norway provided the primary dataset. Statistical analysis (stationarity, autocorrelation, Granger causality) revealed the data captured mostly steady-state operations, insufficient for physics-informed models that require step-change variability.

Niederaussem pilot plant, Germany provided a dynamic experimental campaign with controlled step changes in five process variables. Models were trained directly on this data and it served as the template for synthetic generation.

Model Input Features

  • Upper Water Wash Temperature
  • Upper Water Wash Flow Rate
  • Lean Solvent Flow Rate
  • Lean Solvent Temperature
  • Flue Gas Flow Rate
  • Target: AMP emissions (ppm / mg/m³)

Synthetic Data Generation

S1
Residual Modeling via Vine Copula
Smoothed TCM variable means were subtracted from raw values to obtain residuals. A vine copula was fitted to capture complex multi-variable statistical dependencies.
S2
Step Response Overlay
Copula-sampled residuals were applied to second-order step response functions from each Niederaussem experiment, producing physically realistic synthetic time series.
S3
Fidelity Validation
KS Complement: 0.94. Mantel statistic: 0.29. Best scores among all synthesis approaches tested, including PAR synthesizer.

Model Architecture: Neural Differential Equations

The primary approach embeds a neural network inside a differential equation framework anchored by the second-order step response function from the Charalambous et al. flexible CO2 capture plant literature:

Second-Order Step Response (Charalambous et al., 2021)
y(t) = y₀ + KM [ 1 − (τ₁−ξ)/(τ₁−τ₂) · e−(t−ϑ)/τ₁ − (τ₂−ξ)/(τ₂−τ₁) · e−(t−ϑ)/τ₂ ]

A supplemental neural network (MLP or LSTM) learns residual nonlinear dynamics not captured by the physics backbone, while preserving interpretable parameters: time constants τ, damping ξ, time delay ϑ.

A Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN) served as the baseline model, with both MSE and quantile loss variants to quantify prediction uncertainty.

Figure 1 — NDE data flow. The current system state passes through five system equations and a supplemental neural network; an ODE solver combines outputs to predict the next state.

Analytical Capabilities

Beyond forecasting: understand and act on emission drivers

EmiFor exposes three analytical modes that transform forecasting models into operational decision-support tools for plant engineers and operators.

🔎
Causal Impact Analysis
Isolates the causal effect of a specific operational variable on historical emissions. Granger causality tests remove confounders; a counterfactual baseline is trained on pre-intervention data. Reports Mean, Relative, and Total Impact metrics.
🔄
Counterfactual Evaluation
Answers "what if" questions by modifying historical process variable values and running the model forward to forecast alternative emission trajectories over any user-specified time window.
⚙
Parameter Optimization
Adaptive grid search with iterative refinement finds the process variable combination that minimizes forecasted emissions within user-defined operational constraints. Groundwork for BOED in Phase II.

Demonstration Application

A three-stage workflow any operator can use

A Streamlit proof-of-concept demonstrates real-world deployability. The framework is data-agnostic and accepts any time series, supporting any process governed by differential equations.

1
Data Upload & Preprocessing
Upload any tabular time series, select feature columns, and apply cleaning steps including despiking and exponential window smoothing.
2
Model Selection & Training
Choose a pre-trained PCC-optimized model or train a new one. Automated hyperparameter optimization removes the need for ML expertise. Forecast horizon is configurable.
3
Analytical Exploration
Three interactive pages expose causal impact, counterfactual evaluation, and parameter optimization. Each produces exportable charts and impact metrics.
Figure 5 — Causal impact analysis interface. Users select a variable and intervention window; the app produces emissions projections showing the impact of that feature.
Figure 6 — Counterfactual evaluation interface. Users select a variable, specify a change amount and time window, and the app forecasts emissions under that scenario.
Figure 7 — Parameter optimization UI. Users select process variables and define operational bounds; the app identifies the combination that minimizes forecasted emissions.
Figure 8 — Figma wireframes for the Phase II prototype. Sidebar navigation, persistent sessions, a reporting dashboard, and saved results are introduced while retaining the core analytical workflow.

Phase II Roadmap

Five directions for continued development

→
NDE Integration & Symbolic Regression
Integrate trained NDEs into the production application and apply symbolic regression to derive human-readable mathematical expressions describing emission dynamics.
→
Bayesian Optimal Experimental Design (BOED)
Use Bayesian inference to identify the experiments most likely to yield informative training data, minimizing operational burden while maximizing model improvement.
→
Generalizability Testing
Evaluate the pipeline on smelting, natural gas treatment, and concrete production, targeting methane, VOCs, and sulfur compounds.
→
Progressive Web Application
Replace the Streamlit proof-of-concept with a production PWA offering cross-platform compatibility, persistent sessions, and offline capability.
→
Alpha & Beta Testing
Two-phase user testing with plant operators to eliminate bugs, refine the interface, and validate workflow integration in real carbon capture facilities.
