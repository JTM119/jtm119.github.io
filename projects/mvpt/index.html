MVPT addresses the difficulty of validating AI models against novel, previously unseen data during a space mission. Rather than recomputing validation results on-board in real time, MVPT precomputes a database of expected model performance across a wide variety of data transformations before launch. When an autonomous system encounters new data mid-mission, it retrieves the most similar precomputed result from that database — instantly estimating whether the model will perform reliably without the computational overhead of direct validation.

This increases AI model reliability, allows missions to adapt to changing data conditions, and reduces the computational cost and physical footprint of mission hardware.

TO2 — Proof of concept: DSCOVR solar wind data

The proof-of-concept used solar wind data from the Deep Space Climate Observatory (DSCOVR) spacecraft, recorded at one-minute cadence. The dataset spans years of observations with natural periods of low (quiet) and high (volatile) solar activity. All software was written in Python.

Demonstration scenario: A neural network trained exclusively on quiet solar wind data is asked to forecast the next 30 minutes of magnetic field strength. MVPT estimates in advance how that model will perform when exposed to a volatile solar storm — without rerunning validation on-board.
04

Regime separation

A Hidden Markov Model (HMM) was fitted to rolling volatility features of the magnetic field magnitude to classify each time point as quiet or volatile. Features: first difference of magnetic field magnitude, short and long window rolling standard deviations, and long window variability of the first difference. Post-processing applied a rolling average and minimum-duration constraint to suppress short transient flips.

05

Forecasting model architecture

The model produces probabilistic predictions of magnetic field strength over a 30-minute horizon using a 3-hour historical context window of magnetic field, wind speed, temperature, and proton density. Trained exclusively on quiet data.

1
TCN encoder
Temporal Convolutional Network processes the 3-hour history window. Dilated causal convolutions preserve temporal ordering while capturing patterns at multiple time scales. Produces a compact latent representation summarizing recent system dynamics.
2
Parameter head → time-varying Student-t distribution
Maps the latent representation to coefficients (location, scale, degrees of freedom) of a Student-t distribution evolving across the 30-minute forecast horizon. Student-t chosen for robustness to heavy-tailed solar wind fluctuations. Degrees of freedom controls tail heaviness.
3
Likelihood-based training
Trained by maximizing the likelihood of observed future measurements under the predicted distribution. Couples prediction errors across lead times and promotes stable parameter trajectories across the forecast window.

Data transformations

A large database of synthetic distribution shifts was constructed by applying transformations to the quiet training data. Perturbation combinations limited to order three to maintain computational feasibility (~24 hours of processing on a typical laptop).

ClassMethods
Marginal distributionMean shift, linear drift, variance scaling, heteroscedastic scaling, nonlinear warping, quantile remapping, feature rotations
Temporal structureAutoregressive modification, spectral tilt, band amplification, time warping, block shuffling
Joint distribution (multivariate)Covariance scaling, low-rank perturbations, conditional noise
Structural / supportClipping (sensor saturation), quantization (limited resolution), missingness (sensor dropout / data corruption), outlier bursts

Embeddings and vector lookup

1
Penultimate layer embeddings
Each evaluated dataset is embedded using the penultimate layer activations of the forecasting model, capturing the model's internal functional geometry rather than raw data similarity. Datasets processed similarly by the model will have similar embeddings regardless of surface-level differences.
2
HNSW approximate nearest neighbor index
Embeddings stored in a Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) graph via hnswlib. Normalized vectors enable efficient cosine similarity search across millions of entries. Future deployments may use Qdrant, Milvus, or Weaviate for persistence and distributed scaling.
3
SoftMax-weighted retrieval
For a new dataset, the 100 most similar vectors are retrieved by cosine similarity. Similarity scores are converted to SoftMax weights so closest neighbors contribute most strongly. Weighted averages produce point estimates for NLL, CRPS, and coverage; dispersion among neighbors estimates uncertainty.
07

FCCV computation and final results

The Fraction of Correctly Computed Validations (FCCV) measures how often MVPT correctly predicts whether a model passes or fails validation on a transformed dataset.

Cutoff set at 1.5× the standard deviation of the MAE CRPS distribution — an extremely conservative threshold implying only a 68% probability that MAE CRPS truly exceeds the cutoff. Actual NASA missions would require greater confidence; this conservatism understates true FCCV performance.

The volatile dataset was divided into 20 groups via k-means clustering in the embedding vector space (ensuring independent tests). For each group, MVPT's predicted MAE CRPS was compared against ground truth. If the absolute difference fell within 1.5× the ground truth standard deviation, both values lie on the same side of the cutoff — a match.

Coverage MAE outliers result from large-magnitude transformations (shift >×1000). Normalization in the ML model causes these to produce embeddings that are structurally similar to nominal cases, while being distributionally extreme. Filtering outlier embeddings from the lookup table resolves the issue.

Final FCCV = 0.95 ± 0.05. Assuming the 20 group differences are normally distributed, only 5% of values are expected outside the conservative 1.5× threshold. MVPT meets its benchmark and is technically feasible for estimating the validation state of ML models in autonomous space systems.

Selected: rotorcraft navigation camera (Dragonfly-class)

Signal delay is too long for Earth to react to in-flight issues, requiring full autonomy. Three distinct AI task types can be tested on the same data, and existing datasets and simulation tools are available. The scenario is relevant to upcoming missions including Dragonfly and lunar landers inspecting landing areas.

FROST dataset

11 images of 5m × 5m areas at 4 locations in Death Valley and the Atacama Desert. Each image is 2048 × 2048 pixels with a 2.5mm resolution LiDAR DEM. Unreal Engine 5 will simulate a larger dataset: 500 training images from one location, 25 sets of 20 images as novel test data.

Phase II technical objectives

TO1
Simulate training & mission data
Create a mission-relevant dataset with raw data and ground truth labels. Training data builds MVPT's precomputed validation space; novel mission data with varying properties tests generalization across conditions.
TO2
Validate MVPT accuracy & reliability
Demonstrate both accuracy (closeness of estimated vs. true validation status) and reliability (consistent results across similar but non-identical novel datasets). Multiple sets of similar novel data required.
TO3
Validate computational requirements
Run mission-side MVPT software in an environment mimicking a deep-space computer (e.g., RAD750). Measure memory, storage, and processing time against spacecraft specifications.
TO4
Create mission-ready software package
Ground-side: Python/MATLAB/Julia APIs, auto-managed multiprocessing, user-importable datasets. Mission-side: low-impact, NASA security-compliant, API access in C, C++, and Virtual Machine Language (VML).
